PART 1

The Basics

01What Are Microservices? (vs a Monolith)

A monolith is one big application where all the code — users, orders, payments, notifications — is built and deployed as a single unit. Microservices split that into many small, independent services, each owning one job and deployable on its own.

Layman analogy

A monolith is one giant kitchen cooking every dish — if the dessert station catches fire, the whole kitchen shuts. Microservices are a food court: separate stalls (pizza, sushi, coffee), each with its own staff and till. One stall closing doesn't stop the others, and you can upgrade the coffee stall without touching the pizza one.

MonolithMicroservices
DeployAll at onceEach service independently
ScaleWhole appOnly the busy service
TechOne stackDifferent stack per service
FailureCan take everything downIsolated to one service
ComplexitySimple to startDistributed-systems overhead

02When to Use Them (and When Not To)

Microservices are a solution to scale and team problems, not a default.

  • Use when: large app, many teams that keep stepping on each other, parts that need to scale differently (e.g. checkout gets 10x traffic on sale day), or you need independent release cadence.
  • Avoid when: small app or small team, early-stage product still finding its shape, or you can't yet afford the operational cost (monitoring, CI/CD, on-call).
Honest take

"Start with a well-structured monolith; split into microservices when a real pain (scaling, team friction) appears." Premature microservices add network calls, data-consistency headaches, and ops burden for little benefit.

03How to Split the System

The hard part isn't the code — it's drawing the boundaries. Split by business capability, not by technical layer.

  • By business capability / bounded context (from Domain-Driven Design): Orders, Payments, Inventory, Notifications — each a service.
  • Each service owns its own data and exposes a clear API. No other service reaches into its tables.
  • Aim for high cohesion (related things together) and loose coupling (services barely depend on each other's internals).
Anti-pattern

Don't split by layer (a "database service", a "controller service"). That just spreads one feature across many services — every change touches all of them. This is called a distributed monolith: all the pain, none of the benefit.

PART 2

How Services Talk

04Synchronous vs Asynchronous Communication

Synchronous (REST/gRPC)Asynchronous (messaging)
StyleCall and wait for a replySend a message, move on
CouplingCaller needs callee up nowDecoupled; callee can be down
Use forImmediate answer (get profile)Events, background work (order placed)
Layman analogy

Synchronous = a phone call: you wait on the line for the answer. Asynchronous = texting: you send it and carry on; they reply when they can. Messaging (like Kafka/RabbitMQ) is texting for services — the sender doesn't wait, so a slow or down service can't block the caller.

Rule of thumb

Prefer async events for cross-service workflows — they keep services independent and resilient. Use sync calls only when you truly need an immediate answer.

05The API Gateway

An API Gateway is the single front door for clients. Instead of a mobile app knowing 20 service addresses, it calls one gateway, which routes to the right service.

Layman analogy

It's the receptionist at a big office. You don't wander the building looking for the right person — you tell reception who you need and they direct you. The gateway also checks your ID (auth), stops troublemakers (rate limiting), and logs visitors.

Typical gateway jobs: routing, authentication, rate limiting, request aggregation (combine several service calls into one response), SSL termination, and logging.

06Service Discovery

Services start, stop, and move (especially in the cloud), so their addresses change. Service discovery lets a service find others by name instead of a hard-coded IP.

Layman analogy

A phone directory. You look up "Payments" by name and get its current number, instead of memorising a number that keeps changing. A service registry (e.g. Eureka, Consul, or Kubernetes' built-in DNS) keeps this directory up to date as services come and go.

Paired with a load balancer, discovery also spreads requests across the multiple copies (instances) of a service.

PART 3

Managing Data

07Database per Service

The golden rule: each service owns its own private database. Others may only get that data by calling the service's API — never by querying its tables directly.

Why so strict?

If two services share a database, changing a table breaks both — you've secretly rebuilt the monolith. Private data lets each team evolve their schema freely. The tradeoff: data is now spread out, so you can't just do a JOIN across services.

08Distributed Transactions & the Saga Pattern

In a monolith, one database transaction can update everything atomically. Across services with separate databases, there's no single transaction. The Saga pattern solves this with a sequence of local transactions, each with a compensating action to undo it if a later step fails.

Layman analogy

Booking a holiday: reserve flight → reserve hotel → reserve car. If the car booking fails, you don't have one big "undo" — you cancel the hotel and cancel the flight (compensating actions), step by step, to get back to a clean state.

StyleHow it coordinates
ChoreographyNo central boss; each service reacts to events and emits the next event. Simple, but the flow is spread out and hard to follow.
OrchestrationA central "orchestrator" tells each service what to do next. Clearer flow and easier to debug, but one more component.

09Eventual Consistency & the Outbox Pattern

Since data lives in many services, they can be briefly out of sync but converge shortly after — this is eventual consistency. Most systems accept it (your bank balance updating a few seconds after a transfer is fine).

The dual-write problem: a service often must both save to its DB and publish an event. If it saves but crashes before publishing, the rest of the system never hears about it.

Outbox pattern (the fix)

Write the data and the event into the same database transaction (the event goes into an "outbox" table). A separate process then reads the outbox and publishes the events reliably. Now the save and the "intent to publish" succeed or fail together.

10CQRS & Event Sourcing

  • CQRS (Command Query Responsibility Segregation) — separate the write model from the read model. Writes go to one place; reads come from a separate, query-optimised view. Useful when reads and writes have very different shapes or scale.
  • Event sourcing — instead of storing only the current state, store the full sequence of events. Current state is rebuilt by replaying them.
Layman analogy

Event sourcing is your bank statement: the bank doesn't just store "balance = 500"; it stores every deposit and withdrawal, and the balance is the sum. You get a perfect audit trail and can reconstruct the balance at any past moment.

Caution

These are powerful but add real complexity. Reach for them only when the problem genuinely needs them — not by default.

PART 4

Resilience (Designing for Failure)

11Failure Is Normal — Plan for It

In a distributed system, networks drop, services slow down, and instances die. Good microservices assume failure and contain it. Core techniques:

  • Timeouts — never wait forever for a reply; give up after, say, 2 seconds.
  • Retries with backoff — retry a transient failure, but wait longer each time (and add jitter) so you don't stampede a struggling service.
  • Bulkhead — isolate resources so one failing dependency can't drown the whole service (like watertight compartments in a ship — one floods, the ship stays afloat).
  • Fallback — a graceful degraded response (show cached prices if the pricing service is down).

12The Circuit Breaker

A circuit breaker stops a service from repeatedly calling a dependency that's clearly failing, giving it time to recover and failing fast instead of piling on.

Layman analogy

It's the electrical fuse in your house. If there's a dangerous surge, the breaker trips (cuts the circuit) to prevent a fire, rather than letting current keep flowing into the fault.

CLOSED requests flow normally | too many failures v OPEN fail fast for a cooldown period (don't even try) | after cooldown v HALF-OPEN let a few test requests through success -> back to CLOSED ; failure -> back to OPEN

13Idempotency

With retries and at-least-once messaging, the same request can arrive twice. An idempotent operation produces the same result whether it runs once or five times — so duplicates don't cause double charges.

Layman analogy

A lift (elevator) call button: pressing it five times doesn't call five lifts. The system remembers "already requested."

How

Give each request a unique idempotency key; the service records processed keys and ignores repeats. Essential for payments and any "at-least-once" messaging.

PART 5

Operating Microservices

14Observability (Logs, Metrics, Tracing)

When a request hops through ten services, you need to see what happened. Three pillars:

  • Centralized logging — all services ship logs to one place (e.g. ELK stack) so you search everything together.
  • Metrics — numbers over time (latency, error rate, throughput) via Prometheus + Grafana dashboards.
  • Distributed tracing — a correlation/trace ID attached to a request and passed to every service, so you can follow one request end-to-end (Jaeger, Zipkin).
Layman analogy

The trace ID is a parcel tracking number. One number lets you see every depot the parcel passed through and where it got delayed — without it, a lost parcel among ten depots is a nightmare to trace.

15Deployment: Containers & Orchestration

  • Docker packages a service with everything it needs into a container — "it runs the same on my laptop and in production."
  • Kubernetes orchestrates many containers: scheduling, scaling up/down, restarting crashed ones, and rolling out new versions.
Layman analogy

A container is a shipping container: standard box, moves the same on any truck, ship, or crane. Kubernetes is the port operator deciding where each container goes, adding more when busy, and replacing damaged ones.

Safe release strategies: blue-green (run new version alongside old, then switch traffic) and canary (send a small % of traffic to the new version first, watch, then ramp up).

16Configuration & Security

  • Externalized/central config — keep settings outside the code (a config server or Kubernetes ConfigMaps/Secrets) so you change them without redeploying, and per environment.
  • Security — the API gateway handles authentication; services trust a signed JWT passed along. Internally, services use TLS (encrypted traffic) — often a service mesh (like Istio) handles this transparently.
Zero trust

Don't assume "inside the network = safe." Each service verifies the caller's token and permissions, so a breach of one service doesn't hand over everything.

PART 6

Quick-Fire Q&A

Tap a question to reveal the answer.

>Monolith vs microservices in one line?

A monolith is one deployable unit (one big kitchen); microservices are many small, independently deployable services (a food court of stalls).

>When should you NOT use microservices?

Small apps/teams, early-stage products, or when you can't afford the ops overhead. Start with a good monolith and split when real pain appears.

>How do you decide service boundaries?

By business capability / bounded context, with each service owning its own data — high cohesion, loose coupling. Splitting by technical layer creates a distributed monolith.

>Why database per service?

So teams can change their schema independently; a shared DB re-couples services. The cost is no cross-service joins and needing sagas for consistency.

>What is a Saga?

A sequence of local transactions across services, each with a compensating action to undo it if a later step fails — like cancelling a hotel if the flight booking fails.

>Choreography vs orchestration?

Choreography: services react to each other's events, no central boss. Orchestration: a central coordinator directs each step — clearer but adds a component.

>What problem does the outbox pattern solve?

The dual-write problem — writing to the DB and publishing an event atomically. The event is saved in the same transaction (an outbox table) and published later reliably.

>What is a circuit breaker?

A safety mechanism that stops calling a failing dependency (trips open), fails fast during a cooldown, then tests recovery (half-open) — like an electrical fuse.

>Why is idempotency important?

Retries and at-least-once messaging can deliver duplicates; idempotent operations produce the same result on repeats, preventing double charges. Use idempotency keys.

>How do you debug a request across many services?

Distributed tracing with a correlation/trace ID passed through every service, plus centralized logs and metrics — like a parcel tracking number.

>Sync vs async communication?

Sync (REST/gRPC) waits for a reply (phone call); async (messaging) sends and moves on (texting). Prefer async events for resilient cross-service workflows.

>What is a distributed monolith?

Microservices so tightly coupled (shared DB, layer-based split) that they must be deployed together — all the complexity of distribution with none of the independence.

Final tips for the interview

  1. Frame microservices as a solution to scale/team pain, not a default.
  2. Draw boundaries by business capability; avoid the distributed monolith.
  3. Explain database per service and the eventual-consistency tradeoff.
  4. Know Saga + compensating actions and the outbox pattern.
  5. Cover resilience: timeouts, retries, circuit breaker, idempotency.
  6. Mention tracing, containers, and canary releases for real-world credibility.
